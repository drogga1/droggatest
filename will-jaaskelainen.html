Desc The younger Jääskeläinen is a goalkeeper, just like his father Jussi Jaaskelainen. Twenty-one-year-old Will started out with Bolton, where his old man played more than 500 games, but left the Trotters for Crewe Alexandra in 2017. After a series of loan spells in non-league, he finally made his Crewe debut late last season and has been in goal for the first two games of the League Two season

[The son of former Bolton legend Jussi Jaaskelainen - Will is plying his trade at League Two side Crewe, and hopes to emulate Leicester stopper Kasper Schmeichel by stepping out of his father’s shadow] With a surname like mine, everyone knows who I am - or at least who my dad is. It’s a pretty obvious connection. But I’m proud to follow him into the same trade. He didn’t tell me to be a goalkeeper. He’s always let me make my own decisions. I decided, at a young age, that I wanted to play football like him, and he never saw any reason to stop me

[At 21, Will is still nearer sapling than mighty oak, but his role models are, unsurprisingly, fellow Scandinavians] I look up to Kasper Schmeichel, and what he’s achieved by following in his father’s footsteps, and dream of doing as well as him. Now he’s not just the son of a famous father. He’s one of the best goalkeepers in the world in his own right. And Kasper didn’t start at the top - he gained experience in the lower leagues before he won the title at Leicester , which stood him in good stead. Going out on loan, and playing 60-odd games over two years in non-League football (with Loughborough Dynamos, Nantwich, Buxton and Chester) helped me a lot. You can hear every comment from behind the goal. You don’t just improve as a keeper - it probably sharpens your mind, and maybe you develop a thicker skin. I don’t think hanging around and playing Under-23 games develops you as much as competitive non-League football… the best way to learn from your mistakes is in front of a crowd. It toughens you up
