Tofu is soft. It’s often so soft that it’s difficult to pick up with chopsticks. It easily falls apart.​Aburaage is Japan’s solution to the soft tofu problem. It’s thinly sliced tofu that’s deep fried according to an elaborate process that yields a flexible, tasty, resilient tofu

Aburaage is difficult to make at home and generally is factory produced

Aburaage has been around for at least 200 years. ​Aburaage is unique to Japanese cuisine. It’s used in a variety of traditional dishes: Inarizushi, Miso Soup, Oden, Atsuage, Inari Udon & Inari Soba

Any good miso soup balances ingredients that float and ingredients that sink. Aburaage floats. Regular tofu sinks

Oden is a Japanese winter stew of numerous ingredients stewed for a very long time in a light broth

Atsuage is a thicker variation of aburaage. Its middle is regular tofu and its outer shell is aburaage. Atsuage may be served grilled or in a light broth as a main dish

Inari is a popular Shinto goddess. She is the goddess of everything important including rice, sake, tea, fertility, farming, industry and worldly success. It’s little wonder why she’s popular

foxes get plenty of respect in Japan. There is usually a small shrine dedicated to foxes at Inari shrines … association of Inari to foxes and foxes to aburaage gives it a special status as a food of the gods (kami). Aburaage may be offered to the kami at Shinto shrines
